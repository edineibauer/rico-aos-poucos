Bitcoin: From Original Proposal to 2026 Reality

Bitcoin was born to be a decentralized payment currency. Seventeen years later, it became a speculative asset. This analysis examines Satoshi's proposal, intrinsic value, diminishing cycles and answers: does Bitcoin have a future or is it a bubble waiting to burst?

Jan 06, 2026 18 min read
17 Years
Since Satoshi Nakamoto's whitepaper (2008)

On October 31, 2008, amid the global financial crisis, a pseudonym called Satoshi Nakamoto published a 9-page document titled "Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System".

The proposal was revolutionary: a decentralized digital currency that would eliminate financial intermediaries. Seventeen years later, Bitcoin became a $2 trillion asset, but did it fulfill its original promise?

1. The Original Proposal: Digital Cash for Daily Use

Satoshi's whitepaper had a clear objective: create an "electronic payment system based on cryptographic proof instead of trust". The idea was to replace banks and intermediaries in everyday transactions.

"A purely peer-to-peer version of electronic cash would allow online payments to be sent directly from one party to another without going through a financial institution."
— Satoshi Nakamoto, Bitcoin Whitepaper (2008)

What happened in practice?

In 2025, according to CoinDesk, Bitcoin turned 17 and the narrative changed completely:

  • From payment currency → store of value
  • From decentralization → institutional adoption (ETFs, Wall Street)
  • From cypherpunk rebellion → mainstream speculative asset

Nicholas Gregory, one of Bitcoin's early adopters, summarized: "Bitcoin's transition from peer-to-peer cash to store of value is evident."

The Central Contradiction

Bitcoin was created to be spent daily. Today, the mantra is "never sell your Bitcoin". If nobody spends it, how can it be a functional currency?

2. Bitcoin as Payment Currency: Why It Failed

Prohibitive fees for daily use

Bitcoin transaction fees fluctuate drastically — from $0.37 to over $82 at peak times. In March 2025, during a market correction, fees reached $82.53 in 18 hours of panic.

The El Salvador Case: The Failed Experiment

In 2021, El Salvador became the first country to adopt Bitcoin as legal tender. In January 2025, the country abandoned the experiment after IMF pressure.

According to the IMF analysis: "Bitcoin adoption as legal tender did not contribute to promoting financial inclusion or digital remittances."

3. Why Countries Need Their Own Currency

A fundamental question: does it make sense for a country to adopt Bitcoin as national currency?

The answer from economists and the IMF is consistent: no. Here's why:

Monetary policy is essential

Countries need to adjust money supply to respond to economic crises. With Bitcoin:

  • No interest rate control: Central bank cannot stimulate or slow the economy
  • No "lender of last resort": In banking crises, there's no rescue
  • Structural deflation risk: Limited supply can suffocate growth
  • Extreme volatility: Budget planning becomes impossible
"Monetary policy would lose force. Central banks cannot set interest rates in a foreign currency. Countries normally 'import' the credibility of foreign monetary policy — but this is not possible with cryptoassets."
— IMF, "Cryptoassets as National Currency? A Step Too Far" (2021)

The bitcoiners' counter-argument

Advocates argue that governments abuse money printing, causing inflation. It's true that fiat currencies can be mismanaged. But the solution is improving monetary governance, not eliminating the flexibility that allows crisis response.

Conclusion: Bitcoin doesn't replace fiat currency

Countries need monetary sovereignty to manage their economies. Bitcoin can coexist as a speculative asset, but it doesn't work as a functional national currency.

The Substitutability Problem

Gold cannot be replicated — its physical properties are unique. Bitcoin can be replaced by any other cryptocurrency with similar or better characteristics. USDT, USDC, Ethereum make global transfers with less cost and volatility.

5. The Intrinsic Value Debate

What critics say

"If you told me you owned all the Bitcoin in the world and offered it to me for $25, I wouldn't take it. Because what would I do with it? I'd have to sell it back to you. It isn't going to do anything."
— Warren Buffett (2022)

Steve Hanke (Johns Hopkins): "Bitcoin has zero fundamental value."

Eugene Fama (Nobel Economics): "Bitcoin has close to 100% probability of becoming worthless in the next decade."

Nassim Taleb (author of "Black Swan"): "Bitcoin is a 'technological tulip'. The price is essentially zero."

Peter Schiff (economist): "Bitcoin failed in 2025. While gold and silver hit records, Bitcoin had poor performance. It's a 'slow death'."

The "Greater Fool" Theory

Buffett implicitly describes the "Greater Fool Theory": you buy an asset not for its intrinsic value, but expecting someone to pay more in the future. The problem? Eventually the "fools" willing to pay more run out.

According to Brookings Institution: "Bitcoin has no intrinsic value and is not backed by anything. Devotees claim value comes from scarcity, but scarcity alone is hardly a source of value. Investors seem to rely on the greater fool theory."

The counter-argument: subjective value

Advocates argue that "value is subjective" — it's in the eye of the beholder. The dollar also has no intrinsic value, only trust.

True. But the dollar has:

  • Government that requires it for tax payment
  • $25 trillion economy backing it
  • World's most powerful military behind it
  • Global reserve currency status

Bitcoin has... the belief that other people will keep believing.

6. Diminishing Cycles: The Concerning Pattern

One of bitcoiners' strongest arguments is: "Bitcoin always rises in the long term". But data shows a concerning trend: each cycle yields less.

Post-Halving Returns (Peak vs Previous Low)

2012 Cycle (1st halving) +6,000% to +8,000%
2016 Cycle (2nd halving) +2,000% (peak) / +291% (avg)
2020 Cycle (3rd halving) +541% to +600%
2024 Cycle (4th halving) ~100% (until Oct/2025)

According to Kaiko Research: "One year after the 2024 halving, Bitcoin is between $80,000 and $90,000 — the worst post-halving performance in history in percentage terms."

Why does this happen?

  • Law of large numbers: It's easier to go from $100 to $10,000 (100x) than from $100,000 to $10,000,000 (100x)
  • Market maturation: Bitcoin is the 8th largest asset in the world. Less room for exponential growth
  • Institutional saturation: Those who wanted to buy already bought

The $2 Trillion Question

If the trend continues, the 2028 cycle may have even smaller returns. What if a cycle closes negative? What happens when people realize "Bitcoin always rises" is no longer true?

7. Demand Exhaustion: The Saturation Risk

According to Glassnode and CryptoQuant, there are signs of buyer exhaustion:

  • ETFs shifted from accumulation to distribution in Q4 2025
  • Funding rates in futures at lowest level since December 2023
  • "Supply wall" between $93k-$120k creating resistance
  • Long-term holders selling to institutions (change of hands, not new demand)

CryptoQuant compared the current pattern with late 2021, which preceded the 2022 bear market.

8. Does Bitcoin Generate Real Value?

One way to evaluate assets is to ask: does it generate real value for someone?

  • Stocks: Represent companies that generate profits, products, jobs
  • Real Estate: Generate rent, housing, real utility
  • Bonds: Finance governments and companies, pay interest
  • Gold: Industrial use, jewelry, millennial reserve
  • Ethereum: Gas fees for smart contracts (real demand)
  • Bitcoin: ?

Bitcoin doesn't generate cash flow, has no industrial use, doesn't pay dividends. Its "value" depends entirely on other people wanting to buy.

The difference from Ethereum

Ethereum has a different model: to use the network (smart contracts, DeFi, NFTs), you need to pay fees in ETH. This creates real demand for the token.

Bitcoin doesn't have that utility. You don't need Bitcoin for anything specific — you just speculate that the price will rise.

After detailed analysis, we reached the following conclusions:

  1. Bitcoin doesn't work as payment currency. High fees, extreme volatility and slowness make it impractical. El Salvador's experiment proved this.
  2. Bitcoin doesn't replace fiat currency. Countries need monetary sovereignty to manage their economies. No serious country will give that up.
  3. The gold comparison is fragile. Gold has millennial history, industrial use and behaves as safe haven in crises. Bitcoin behaves as risk asset and is replaceable by other cryptos.
  4. Intrinsic value is zero. Bitcoin doesn't generate cash flow, has no essential functional utility. Its price depends 100% on new buyers.
  5. Cycles are weakening. From +8,000% to +100%. The law of large numbers and saturation are taking their toll.
  6. Saturation risk is real. When everyone who wants to buy has already bought, what sustains the price?
Bearish
Our long-term sentiment on Bitcoin

The big question

Can it rise more in the short term? Yes, speculation can continue for 1-2 more cycles.

Is it structurally sound in the long term? Our analysis suggests not. When the "always rises" narrative breaks, the lack of real utility will be exposed.

When does this happen? Impossible to predict. Could be 2030, could be 2040. But assets without real utility eventually find their fundamental value — which in Bitcoin's case, according to critics, is close to zero.
