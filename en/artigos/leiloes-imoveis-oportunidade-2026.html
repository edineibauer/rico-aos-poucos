If you follow economic news, you've noticed something different is happening in the Brazilian real estate market. Property auctions are at historic highs — and this represents a unique opportunity for those who know how to take advantage.

As we explained in our article about Inflation: The Invisible Tax, the government's main mechanism to fight inflation is raising interest rates. And when rates go up, many families and companies with debts tied to the Selic rate simply can no longer pay.

The result? Properties being repossessed and sold at auction for prices well below market value. And this phenomenon is just beginning.

2. Why So Many Properties at Auction?

The answer lies in the combination of three factors that have intensified since the pandemic:

The Perfect Storm

High inflation → High interest rates → Unaffordable payments → Defaults → Property repossession → Mass auctions

2.1. Rising Defaults

According to Central Bank data, mortgage defaults:

  • Peaked at 1.54% in 2023 (highest since 2017)
  • In FGTS-backed loans, jumped from 1.99% (2019) to 2.63% (2023)
  • Brazil now has 77.8 million people in debt (Serasa data, June 2025)

2.2. The Effect of High Interest Rates

When interest rates rise, payments on variable-rate loans spike. A practical example:

Interest Rate Impact on a R$ 300,000 Mortgage

Payment at 2% Selic (2020) ~R$ 2,100
Payment at 14% Selic (2025) ~R$ 3,800
Increase +81%

Many families simply couldn't absorb this increase and had their properties repossessed.

4. How It Works in Practice

The process of buying a property at auction follows these steps:

1

Find the Auction

Follow official auctioneer websites, Caixa Econômica, Banco do Brasil, and platforms like SuperBid, Sold, Zukerman.

2

Analyze the Notice

The notice is the most important document. It lists: minimum value, debts, occupancy status, payment terms, and deadlines.

3

Check the Title

Request an updated title from the Property Registry. Check for liens, mortgages, or other encumbrances.

4

Visit the Property

Whenever possible, visit the property in person. Check condition, actual location, and if it's occupied.

5

Calculate Total Costs

Beyond the bid, consider: transfer tax (2-3%), registration (~1%), any property tax and condo debts, eviction and renovation costs.

6

Participate in the Auction

Register on the platform, qualify, and place your bids. Set a maximum limit and don't get carried away by emotion.

7

Pay and Regularize

After winning, pay according to notice terms, register the award letter, and arrange eviction if necessary.

5.1. The Eviction Problem

One of the biggest challenges is when the property is occupied by the former owner or tenant. The eviction process follows these steps:

  1. Friendly negotiation: Always try first. Offer a reasonable deadline (30-60 days) and, if possible, help with moving.
  2. Extrajudicial notice: If there's no agreement, send a formal notice via notary.
  3. Possession action: As a last resort, file a lawsuit. This can take 6 months to 2 years.

Important Statistic

Studies show that about 35% of properties in extrajudicial auctions generate some legal dispute involving possession or unexpected debts. Be prepared.

6. Why Buy in Your Region?

One of the most important pieces of advice for auction investors: buy properties where you know the market.

Advantages of Buying Locally

  • You know the good and problematic neighborhoods
  • You know the price per square meter in the area
  • You can easily visit the property before the auction
  • You can monitor work and solve problems quickly
  • You have a network of contacts (agents, lawyers, contractors)
  • You know the demand: if it rents fast, if it sells quick

Risks of Buying Far Away

  • Don't know the real local market demand
  • Difficulty visiting and monitoring the property
  • Extra costs for travel and management
  • Can be misled about the actual location
  • Eviction problems are more complex
  • Renovations are more expensive and harder to supervise
"Don't buy properties where you wouldn't walk. If you don't know the area, the risk of overpaying for something worthless increases exponentially."
— Classic advice from real estate investors

7. Calculating Returns

The great advantage of auctions is the margin of safety. See a practical example:

Example: Apartment at Auction

Market value R$ 400,000
Winning bid (40% discount) R$ 240,000
Transfer tax + Registration (~4%) R$ 9,600
Debts (taxes + condo) R$ 15,000
Renovation R$ 25,000
Total Cost R$ 289,600
Potential Profit on Sale R$ 110,400 (38%)

Of course, this is an optimistic scenario. Costs can be higher, sales can take longer, and surprises happen. That's why it's important to:

  1. Never pay more than 60-70% of market value
  2. Have a contingency reserve (minimum 10% of value)
  3. Consider the cost of time (your money will be tied up)

After analyzing the data and current scenario, our conclusions are:

  1. The timing is exceptional. The combination of high interest rates, growing defaults, and large property supply creates an opportunity window we haven't seen in decades.
  2. It's not for everyone. Auctions require available capital, patience, basic legal knowledge, and willingness to deal with bureaucracy and surprises.
  3. Buy in your region. The competitive advantage of knowing the local market is enormous. Don't risk buying properties in cities you've never visited.
  4. Prefer judicial auctions. The additional legal security compensates for the greater bureaucracy, especially for beginners.
  5. Do the math right. The discount needs to cover all costs (debts, renovation, fees) and still leave a profit margin. If the numbers don't work, move on.
  6. Have a reserve. Surprises happen. Have at least 10-15% of the property value in reserve for emergencies.
