The Market Already Priced It In: Why Following Consensus Can Be a Mistake

When everyone is buying the same thing, it's probably too late. Understand the mechanics of pricing and why "not losing" can be more important than "winning more".

Summary: Financial markets are forward-looking — they anticipate future events and incorporate them into prices before they happen. When an expectation becomes consensus, it's already reflected in prices. Making money consistently requires identifying what hasn't been priced in yet, not following the herd.

1. The Efficient Market Hypothesis

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), developed by Eugene Fama in the 1960s, states that asset prices reflect all available information. In other words: if information is public and known, it's already incorporated in the price.

"If the stock price does not already reflect that information, then investors can trade on it, thereby moving the price until the information is no longer useful for trading."

— Wikipedia: Efficient Market Hypothesis

The direct implication is devastating for those who follow consensus: it's impossible to "beat the market" consistently by trading on public information, because that information is already in the prices.

The Three Forms of Efficiency

  • Weak Form: Past prices don't predict future prices (technical analysis doesn't work)
  • Semi-Strong Form: All public information is already in prices (fundamental analysis generates no alpha)
  • Strong Form: Even insider information is already reflected (practically impossible)

The real market operates somewhere between weak and semi-strong form. The crucial point: consensus expectations are public information. If everyone expects rates to fall, that expectation is already in asset prices.

2. The TLT Case: Concrete Proof

Want real proof that markets anticipate events? Look at TLT (iShares 20+ Year Treasury Bond ETF), which invests in long-term US Treasury bonds.

The TLT Paradox in 2024-2025

  • The Fed cut rates by 100 basis points during 2024
  • Long-term bonds should have risen in price (rates fall → prices rise)
  • Actual result: TLT had a NEGATIVE return of -7.5% for the year
  • The 10-year Treasury yield rose from 3.88% to 4.61%

How is this possible? The answer lies in advance pricing:

"The moves in TLT raise the question of how much Fed easing is already priced into bond markets. [...] One could make the case that the 10-year Treasury yield at 3.6% and a 30-year Treasury yield at 3.9% already reflect that."

— ETF.com: TLT Sinks - Are Fed Rate Cuts Priced In?

The market doesn't wait for the Fed to announce cuts. It anticipates. When the cut finally happens, the news is already "old" — prices moved months before.

3. Gold Up 65%: Now What?

Gold had a spectacular 2025: +65% appreciation, the best year since the Jimmy Carter era. It surpassed $4,000/oz and broke over 50 all-time records. And now everyone recommends buying gold "for protection".

The Question Nobody Asks

If gold already rose 65% because the market anticipated crises and instability, how much of that protection is already in the price?

According to the World Gold Council:

"The gold price broadly reflects macroeconomic consensus expectations and may remain rangebound if current conditions persist."

This doesn't mean gold can't go higher. JPMorgan projects $5,000/oz by end of 2026. But the point is: buying protection after everyone else has bought is riskier than it seems.

4. The Danger of Consensus in Brazil

In Brazil 2026, the market consensus says:

  • "Selic will fall to 12% by year end"
  • "Buy stocks and REITs to ride the rate cuts"
  • "Those who stay in CDI will lose money"

If you understood the logic so far, you already see the problem: if everyone expects Selic at 12%, that expectation is already in stock and REIT prices. The rate cut alone won't generate the extraordinary returns that influencers promise.

According to Eurasia Group and Goldman Sachs, there are significant risks that could reverse the narrative.

6. The Risk of IPCA+ Bonds

Another dangerous consensus: "Buy Treasury IPCA+ 2035/2045 and you'll gain a lot from mark-to-market when rates fall." This may be true — or it could be a trap.

Duration Risk

Long IPCA+ bonds have high duration — they're extremely sensitive to rate changes. If the market starts demanding IPCA+9% or IPCA+10% to finance the government, your bond bought at IPCA+7% could drop 30-40% in price.

7. Buffett's Rule #1

Warren Buffett, the greatest investor in history, has a simple philosophy:

"The first rule of an investment is don't lose [money]. And the second rule of an investment is don't forget the first rule. And that's all the rules there are."

— Warren Buffett via Yahoo Finance

The Math of Loss

  • If you lose 50%, you need to gain 100% to break even
  • If you lose 30%, you need to gain 43% to recover
  • If you never lose, any positive gain makes you progress

The goal is not to maximize gains. It's to minimize the chance of catastrophic loss.

8. Conclusion: The Strategy of Not Losing

The Prudent Strategy for 2026

  • Solid base in CDI/Treasury Selic: Enjoy 15% per year with safety
  • Limited risk exposure: If you want to bet on stocks/REITs, use only money you can lose
  • IPCA+ with caution: Prefer shorter maturities or be prepared to hold until the end
  • Dollar protection: Keep 15-20% in dollar assets as a hedge against Brazilian crisis
  • Expect the unexpected: Real money is made when the market is wrong about something, not when it's right

If the optimistic scenario happens, you gain less than you could. That's ok — you miss opportunities every day.

If the pessimistic scenario happens, you're protected, with money available to buy cheap assets.

The goal is not to get rich quick. It's to not get poor.
