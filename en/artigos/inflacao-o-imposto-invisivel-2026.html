Inflation: The Invisible Tax Eroding Your Money

You've probably heard about inflation. You know that prices rise over time. But do you really understand its impact on your money? More importantly: do you know that official inflation (IPCA in Brazil) doesn't reflect the real inflation you feel in your wallet?

This article will clearly explain what inflation is, why it exists, and reveal how governments calculate a number that systematically underestimates actual price increases. By the end, you'll know how to estimate a more realistic inflation rate using official data.

1. What Is Inflation? Understand It Once and For All

Inflation is the loss of purchasing power of money over time. In other words: the same amount of money buys fewer things in the future than it does today.

It's not that things are getting "more expensive" — it's that money is losing value. This distinction is fundamental to understanding the mechanics behind the phenomenon.

Practical Example: Price of Bread (50g roll) in Brazil

1994 R$ 0.12
2004 R$ 0.25 +108%
2014 R$ 0.50 +317%
2024 R$ 1.00 +733%
2026 R$ 1.20 +900%

If you had R$ 100 in 1994, you could buy approximately 833 bread rolls. In 2026, the same R$ 100 buys only 83 rolls. Your money lost 90% of its purchasing power just for this item.

The "Invisible Tax"

Inflation is called an "invisible tax" because it transfers wealth from savers to debtors. If you keep cash, it loses value. If you owe money, your debt "shrinks" in real terms. The largest debtor in any country is the government — that's why it benefits from inflation.

2. Why Does Inflation Exist?

Inflation isn't a natural phenomenon — it's caused primarily by expansion of the money supply. When the government or banking system creates more money than the economy produces goods and services, prices rise.

The Main Causes

Causes of Inflation

Monetary Expansion Primary cause
Fiscal Deficit Government spends more than it collects
Supply Shock Product scarcity
Currency Devaluation Weaker currency = expensive imports
Expectations Self-fulfilling prophecies

Economist Milton Friedman, Nobel Prize winner, summarized it this way:

"Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon, in the sense that it is and can be produced only by a more rapid increase in the quantity of money than in output."
— Milton Friedman, Nobel Prize in Economics (1976)

Why Do Governments "Accept" Inflation?

Although inflation harms the population, especially the poor, it brings some "benefits" to governments:

  • Reduces the real value of public debt: An indebted government benefits when inflation erodes the value of what it owes
  • Increases nominal tax revenue: Income and consumption taxes grow along with prices
  • Stimulates short-term consumption: If money will lose value, people spend now
  • Masks structural problems: It's easier to "print money" than to make unpopular reforms

3. How Is CPI Calculated?

The IPCA (Broad National Consumer Price Index) is Brazil's official inflation index, calculated by IBGE (Brazilian statistics bureau). It measures price variation of a "basket" of products and services consumed by families earning 1 to 40 minimum wages.

IBGE collects prices in 16 metropolitan areas across Brazil and calculates a weighted average based on each category's weight in family budgets.

4. Why CPI Doesn't Reflect Real Inflation

Here's the crucial point of this article: CPI is a methodological construct that systematically underestimates the inflation you feel day to day. It's not deliberate manipulation, but methodological choices that result in an "optimistic" index.

4.1. The Product Substitution Problem

When a product becomes too expensive, statistics bureaus assume you will substitute it for a cheaper one. For example:

  • If beef rises 30%, they assume you'll buy chicken
  • If mozzarella cheese rises too much, they assume you'll buy another type of cheese
  • If private school becomes too expensive, they assume you'll switch to public

The Obvious Problem

This "substitution" represents a reduction in standard of living, not maintenance! You're eating chicken when you wanted beef. This should be captured as real inflation, but it isn't.

4.2. Quality Adjustments (Hedonic Adjustments)

If a product improves in quality, even at the same price, statistics bureaus may record this as "deflation". Examples:

  • A $1,000 phone in 2024 is "better" than a $1,000 phone in 2020, so CPI considers there was deflation
  • A car with more standard features "justifies" a higher price without counting as inflation
  • Faster internet for the same price = deflation in the index

The problem? You can't buy the "worse" version at the old price. It simply doesn't exist anymore. You're forced to pay more for the "upgrade."

4.3. Outdated Consumption Basket

CPI weights are based on Consumer Expenditure Surveys, updated only every 5-8 years. This means the basket may not reflect how you actually spend your money today.

Item CPI Weight Real Weight (Est.) Problem

4.4. Exclusion of Volatile Items

Central Banks frequently use "core" inflation, which excludes volatile items like food and energy. But these are precisely the items that weigh most heavily in low-income family budgets!

4.5. IGP-M as Comparison

The IGP-M (General Market Price Index), calculated by FGV, historically shows higher inflation than IPCA. This is because it includes wholesale prices and construction, which tend to rise more.

5. How to Calculate More Realistic Inflation?

There are ways to estimate more realistic inflation using official data. Here are the methodologies:

5.1. Method 1: CPI + Correction Factor

Studies by FGV and independent economists suggest that perceived inflation is, on average, 15-25% higher than official CPI. You can use:

CPI × 1.17
Suggested correction factor for real inflation

If CPI was 6% for the year, estimated real inflation would be: 6% × 1.17 = 7.02%

5.2. Method 2: Average of Multiple Indices

A simple way to get a more realistic number is to take the average between different inflation indices:

Average Inflation Calculation (Brazil)

IPCA 2025 ~5.5%
IGP-M 2025 ~8.5%
Average 7.0%

5.3. Method 3: Alternative Indices

The IPC-S (Weekly Consumer Price Index) from FGV and the INPC (National Consumer Price Index) from IBGE, which focuses on lower-income families (1-5 minimum wages), often show higher inflation than IPCA.

5.4. Method 4: Personal Inflation

Many central banks offer a personal inflation calculator where you can enter your actual expenses and calculate your own inflation rate. In the US, you can track this through the BLS.

Now that you understand the difference between official and real inflation, see how it affects your investments:

The Inflation-Linked Bond Trap

Inflation-linked bonds protect against official inflation, not real inflation. If real inflation is 1-2% higher than CPI, your real gain is less than it appears. Still, they're one of the best options for protection.

8. How to Protect Yourself from Inflation

Knowing that inflation erodes your money, what should you do? Here are practical strategies:

8.1. Investments That Protect

  • Inflation-Linked Bonds: Partial protection (uses official CPI)
  • Real Estate / REITs: Properties tend to follow inflation
  • Stocks with Pricing Power: Companies that can pass on costs
  • Dollar/Foreign Assets: Protection against local currency devaluation
  • Gold: Historical store of value against inflation

8.2. What to Avoid

  • Cash sitting in account: Loses value every day
  • Low-yield savings: Returns less than real inflation
  • Low-rate liquid CDs: Real gain close to zero
  • Long-term fixed rate bonds: If inflation rises, you lose

After analyzing the data and methodologies, our conclusions are:

  1. Inflation is an invisible tax that constantly erodes your wealth. Idle money is money losing value.
  2. CPI underestimates real inflation due to product substitution, quality adjustments, and outdated baskets. The inflation you feel is higher.
  3. Use CPI × 1.17 or average of indices to get a more realistic estimate in your investment calculations.
  4. "Inflation-linked" investments protect only partially, as they use official CPI. Still, they're better than nothing.
  5. International diversification (dollar, foreign assets) is important for protection against inflation and local currency devaluation.
  6. Time is your enemy when it comes to inflation. The sooner you start investing, the less inflation can erode your wealth.
