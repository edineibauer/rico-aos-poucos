The Market Already Priced It In: Why Following Consensus Can Be a Mistake

When everyone is buying the same thing, it's probably too late. Understand the mechanics of pricing and why "not losing" can be more important than "winning more".

Jan 14, 2026 18 min read

Summary

Financial markets are forward-looking — they anticipate future events and incorporate them into prices before they happen. When an expectation becomes consensus, it's already reflected in prices. Making money consistently requires identifying what hasn't been priced in yet, not following the herd.

1. The Efficient Market Hypothesis

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), developed by Eugene Fama in the 1960s, states that asset prices reflect all available information. In other words: if information is public and known, it's already incorporated in the price.

"If the stock price does not already reflect that information, then investors can trade on it, thereby moving the price until the information is no longer useful for trading."

— Wikipedia: Efficient Market Hypothesis

The direct implication is devastating for those who follow consensus: it's impossible to "beat the market" consistently by trading on public information, because that information is already in the prices.

The Three Forms of Efficiency

  • Weak Form: Past prices don't predict future prices (technical analysis doesn't work)
  • Semi-Strong Form: All public information is already in prices (fundamental analysis generates no alpha)
  • Strong Form: Even insider information is already reflected (practically impossible)

2. The TLT Case: Concrete Proof

Want real proof that markets anticipate events? Look at TLT (iShares 20+ Year Treasury Bond ETF), which invests in long-term US Treasury bonds.

The TLT Paradox in 2024-2025

  • The Fed cut rates by 100 basis points during 2024
  • Long-term bonds should have risen in price (rates fall → prices rise)
  • Actual result: TLT had a NEGATIVE return of -7.5% for the year
  • The 10-year Treasury yield rose from 3.88% to 4.61%

"The moves in TLT raise the question of how much Fed easing is already priced into bond markets."

— ETF.com

5. Buffett's Rule #1

"The first rule of an investment is don't lose [money]. And the second rule of an investment is don't forget the first rule."

— Warren Buffett

The Math of Loss

  • If you lose 50%, you need to gain 100% to break even
  • If you lose 30%, you need to gain 43% to recover
  • If you never lose, any positive gain makes you progress

6. Conclusion: The Strategy of Not Losing

The Prudent Strategy for 2026

  • Solid base in CDI/Treasury Selic: Enjoy 15% per year with safety
  • Limited risk exposure: If you want to bet on stocks/REITs, use only money you can lose
  • IPCA+ with caution: Prefer shorter maturities or be prepared to hold until the end
  • Dollar protection: Keep 15-20% in dollar assets as a hedge

The goal is not to get rich quick. It's to not get poor.
