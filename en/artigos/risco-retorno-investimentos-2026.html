Risk and Return: What No One Tells You About Investing

Why do people gamble despite terrible odds? When does it make sense to take more risk? The truth about risk and return that could change how you invest.

There's No Free Lunch
Markets price risk ‚Äî higher potential returns always mean higher risk

In our article "How Long Does It Take to Get Rich by Investing?", we showed an uncomfortable truth: with modest contributions and conservative investments, it takes decades to build significant wealth.

Facing this reality, many investors ask the obvious question: "What if I take more risk to earn faster?" The answer isn't simple ‚Äî and that's exactly what we'll discuss.

1. The Fundamental Principle: Risk and Return Go Together

There's an almost universal law in financial markets: the higher the potential return, the higher the risk involved. This isn't coincidence ‚Äî it's the market working.

Think about it: thousands of people analyze every asset in the market. If a stock could deliver 50% returns without risk, everyone would buy it, the price would rise, and potential returns would fall. The market self-adjusts.

"There's no such thing as a free lunch in the markets. If something seems too good to be true, it probably is."
‚Äî Milton Friedman, Nobel Prize in Economics

Why do "cheap" or "expensive" assets exist?

When you see an asset that seems "very cheap" or "very expensive," remember: the market has already priced this in. Thousands of analysts, funds, and algorithms have studied that asset. The price reflects:

  • Real risk: Probability of loss or volatility
  • Liquidity: How easy it is to buy and sell
  • Outlook: Future expectations of growth or decline
  • Information: Everything publicly known about the asset

Beware of "Unique Opportunities"

If someone offers an investment with "guaranteed 5% monthly returns," ask yourself: why are they offering this to you instead of investing themselves? The answer usually involves scam or pyramid scheme.

2. The Math of Risk-Return

To understand if taking a risk is worth it, you need to evaluate two factors:

Risk-Return Components

Expected return How much you can gain (on average)
Probability of success Real chance of it working out
Maximum loss How much you can lose
Expected value Return x Probability - Loss x (1-Prob)

Notice: Lotteries and betting have negative expected value. This means that, on average, you lose money. The house always wins ‚Äî that's how these businesses work.

The logic behind gambling

When you have little money left over, traditional math seems discouraging. As we showed in the previous article, investing $100/month takes 60+ years to reach $200,000.

Then comes the thought:

"If I can put in $20 and have a chance to walk away with $20,000, why not try? If I lose, I'll recover it in a week of work."
‚Äî Common reasoning among gamblers

This reasoning has emotional logic, but it ignores a crucial problem: the negative expected value. In the long run, you always lose to the house.

The Powerball Problem

Probability of hitting jackpot 1 in 292 million
Cost per ticket $2.00
Expected return per $1 spent ~$0.50
You'd need to live... ~5.6 million years playing weekly

The probability is so absurdly low that it doesn't make mathematical sense. It's entertainment, not investment.

4. When Does It Make Sense to Take More Risk?

Here comes the controversial ‚Äî but honest ‚Äî part. There's logic to taking more risk when you have less:

The Logic of Asymmetric Risk

If you can contribute $100/month and risk that amount on something with potential to multiply by 100x, the loss ($100) you recover in 1 month. But the gain ($10,000) would take years to accumulate conservatively.

This doesn't mean gamble on lotteries! It means seeking high-risk assets with positive expected value:

  • Small-cap stocks ‚Äî more volatile, but with growth potential
  • Cryptocurrencies with fundamentals ‚Äî not memecoins, but serious projects
  • Startups (via equity crowdfunding) ‚Äî most fail, but some multiply
  • Investing in yourself ‚Äî courses, own business, skills

Why are billionaires conservative?

Have you noticed that billionaires generally have very diversified and conservative portfolios? Warren Buffett keeps billions in government bonds. Why?

Because when you've already won the game, the goal isn't to earn more ‚Äî it's not to lose. The cost of a catastrophic loss is much greater than the benefit of extra gains.

"Rule number 1: never lose money. Rule number 2: never forget rule number 1."

7. How to Evaluate Risk-Return of Any Investment

Before investing in anything, ask these questions:

  1. What's the realistic expected return? (Not the best case, the average)
  2. What's the probability of success? (Based on data, not promises)
  3. What's the maximum possible loss? (Can you lose everything?)
  4. How long do I need to wait? (Liquidity)
  5. Can I recover this amount if I lose? (Recovery capacity)

Red Flags

Be suspicious if someone offers: guaranteed returns above market rates, "unique opportunity," pressure to invest quickly, promise of gains without risk, or returns way above market average.

After analyzing the relationship between risk and return, our conclusions are:

  1. There's no free lunch. Markets price risk ‚Äî high returns always come with high risk.
  2. Gambling and lotteries have negative expected value. You lose to the house in the long run. It's entertainment, not strategy.
  3. Taking more risk when you have less makes sense ‚Äî but in assets with positive expected value, not gambling.
  4. As wealth grows, protect more. The pain of losing $500,000 is much greater than the joy of gaining another $500,000.
  5. Invest in yourself first. As we showed in the article about how long it takes to get rich, increasing your income has much higher returns than any investment.
  6. Evaluate risk-return of everything. Ask yourself: what's the expected return, what's the probability, and can I recover if it goes wrong?
Conscious Risk
Take risks where math favors you, protect what you've already conquered

The final message

We're not saying never take risks. We're saying take risks intelligently. Understand the math, evaluate your life stage, and make conscious decisions.

If you're starting out and have little, you can take more risk in assets with real potential ‚Äî not gambling. If you've already built wealth, your priority is not losing what you've earned.

And remember: the best investment with the lowest risk and highest return is still investing in yourself.
